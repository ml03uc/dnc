⚖️ Laws & Regulations

The legal framework governing outbound sales calls in the United States.
⚠️
Individual Liability Risk

Individual sales reps can be personally named in TCPA lawsuits. "I didn't know" is not a defense.

📋 Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA) — 1991

A federal law restricting telemarketing calls, auto-dialed calls, prerecorded voice messages, and SMS/text messages to consumers.

  • Requires prior express written consent for marketing calls/texts to cell phones via an ATDS (auto-dialer)
  • Prohibits calling numbers on the National DNC Registry without express written consent
  • Enforced by the FCC — individuals have a private right to sue
  • Damages: $500 per violation, up to $1,500 for willful violations
  • No cap on class action damages — a single campaign can generate millions in liability

📋 Telemarketing Sales Rule (TSR) — FTC

  • Requires telemarketers to check the National DNC Registry before calling — every 31 days
  • Prohibits calls to registered numbers unless specific exemptions apply (EBR, written consent)
  • Mandates maintaining an internal DNC list and honoring opt-outs within 30 days
  • Requires clear identification: disclose company name and purpose of call on every call
  • Restricts calling hours to 8 AM – 9 PM in the called party's local time

🗺️ State DNC Laws

  • Many states have stricter rules than federal law — always apply whichever is stricter
  • California (CCPA/CIPA): Broad consumer rights; express consent required for many contact types
  • Florida: Separate Florida DNC Registry; significant per-call fines
  • Texas: Additional state DNC list; aggressive enforcement
  • When in doubt about a state-specific requirement, escalate to Compliance before calling

📋 The National DNC Registry

What it is, how it works, and your obligations as an outbound caller.
ℹ️
What is it?

A free FTC database at donotcall.gov where consumers register their numbers to opt out of telemarketing. Over 245 million numbers are registered.

🔍 Who Must Comply (DNC Registry)

  • Any company making telemarketing calls to U.S. consumers
  • B2C (Business-to-Consumer) outbound callers
  • B2B (Business-to-Business) calls are generally exempt from the National DNC Registry — it covers residential/personal numbers, not business lines
  • Companies using third-party consumer lead lists must still scrub

📌 Key Requirements

  • Scrub call lists every 31 days
  • Purchase access to the FTC registry (legally required)
  • Keep scrubbing records for at least 5 years
  • Never call a registered number without consent

🚫 Exemptions (When You CAN Call a Registered Number)

  • Established Business Relationship (EBR): Customer purchased within 18 months OR made inquiry within 3 months (applies to landlines under TSR only — see consent module for cell phones)
  • Express Written Consent: Consumer specifically agreed in writing to receive marketing calls from your company
  • Non-commercial calls: Charitable solicitations, political calls, surveys (check state law)
  • B2B calls: Exempt from the National DNC Registry — but TCPA cell phone rules still apply. Auto-dialing a business contact's cell phone still requires prior express consent
⚠️
EBR ≠ TCPA Cell Phone Consent

The EBR exemption applies under TSR for landlines. It does NOT satisfy TCPA requirements for auto-dialed marketing calls to cell phones. You need separate written consent for cell phones.

✅ Consent Requirements

Understanding what constitutes valid consent for outbound calls and text messages.
📝

Express Written Consent

Required for marketing calls/texts to cell phones via auto-dialer or prerecorded message. Must be a clear affirmative agreement.

🗣️

Prior Express Consent

For informational/transactional calls (not marketing). Consumer provided their number in connection with the purpose of the call.

✅ What Makes Consent Valid?

  • Consumer must clearly and affirmatively agree to receive calls or texts
  • Must be in writing (electronic signature counts) for marketing calls to cell phones
  • Consent language must clearly name your company specifically
  • Cannot be a condition of purchase — consent must be completely voluntary
  • Must include the consumer's phone number
  • Must be documented and retained — you must be able to prove it on demand
  • Consent obtained through a third-party lead must specifically name Alternative Payments

❌ What Does NOT Constitute Consent

  • A business card or contact from a trade show
  • An email address or general web form without explicit consent language
  • Replying to a cold email saying they're "interested"
  • A LinkedIn connection or message
  • An EBR (established business relationship) alone — for cell phones
  • A purchased lead list without documented consent records
⚠️
B2B Does NOT Mean TCPA-Free

Alternative Payments sells to businesses, so you might assume TCPA doesn't apply. It does. B2B calls are exempt from the National DNC Registry — but if you are using an auto-dialer (like Apollo) to call a prospect's cell phone, prior express written consent is still required under TCPA, regardless of whether they are a business contact.

🚨
When in Doubt — Don't Call

If you cannot confirm documented written consent for a cell phone number, do not auto-dial it for marketing. Dial manually if you have a legitimate reason to reach them, or escalate to Compliance.

🏢 Internal DNC Policy

Our company-specific rules — designed to exceed federal minimums.
✅
We Go Beyond the Law

Our internal policy exceeds federal requirements. Always follow internal policy first — it is stricter than the legal minimum.

📋 Internal DNC List Requirements

  • Opt-out requests logged in CRM within 24 hours (federal law requires 30 days)
  • DNC covers all channels: phone, mobile, and SMS
  • DNC records retained for a minimum of 5 years
  • All lists scrubbed against internal DNC before any outreach campaign
  • Third-party purchased lists scrubbed before first use — no exceptions

🛑 Handling Opt-Out Requests

When someone tells you not to call — this is your step-by-step guide.
🛑
Opt-Outs Are Immediate and Final

The moment a person says "don't call me," you must stop. You cannot continue to pitch. The opt-out is effective immediately.

📞 Step-by-Step: When Someone Opts Out

1

Acknowledge immediately

Say: "Absolutely, I'll remove you from our call list right away. I apologize for the inconvenience." Do not continue your pitch.

2

Opt them out in Apollo within 24 hours

Use the "···" menu on their Apollo profile to select "Opt out of calls" — do not wait until end of day or week.

3

Notify your manager

Inform your team lead so suppression can be processed at the campaign level and all active sequences are stopped.

4

Never re-add to campaigns

Once marked DNC, the contact must not appear in any future outreach. Do not manually "re-activate" them — ever.

⚠️ Common Mistakes That Create Liability

  • Continuing to pitch after hearing "take me off your list"
  • Waiting to log the DNC until end of week or month
  • Assuming an opt-out applies only to your personal calls, not the whole company
  • Thinking the opt-out only covers one phone number (it typically covers the person)
  • Re-enrolling the contact in a different campaign without clearing the DNC flag

📘 Practice Scenarios

Scenario 1

A prospect says during your call: "Please don't call me anymore — I'm not interested." You think they might change their mind next quarter, so you don't add them to DNC right away.

✅ Correct Action: Opt them out in Apollo immediately using the "···" menu. You cannot defer. Any future call could result in a $1,500 TCPA violation — per call.
Scenario 2

You receive a list of 500 leads from a third-party vendor. Your manager says "start dialing." There's no documentation of consent for mobile numbers on the list.

✅ Correct Action: Stop. Escalate to Compliance before dialing. Companies are liable for calls made from purchased lists. Never assume a vendor's list is TCPA-compliant.
Scenario 3

It's 9:15 PM your local time (Eastern), but the prospect you're about to call is in California where it's 6:15 PM Pacific. Is this call permitted?

✅ Correct Action: Yes — 6:15 PM Pacific is within the 8 AM–9 PM window. Call timing is based on the called party's local time, not yours.
Scenario 4

A prospect gave you their cell phone number on a trade show badge scan 6 months ago. You want to add them to an auto-dialed marketing sequence. Is this okay?

✅ Correct Action: No. A badge scan does not constitute TCPA express written consent. You need explicit consent language specifically for marketing calls from Alternative Payments. Dial manually only, and use consent-based opt-in processes for auto-dial sequences.

🔵 Apollo.io: Opt Out of Calls

How to manually flag a contact as Do Not Call in Apollo, and what to do when you find one that's already been opted out.
ℹ️
Our approach at Alternative Payments

We use Apollo's manual "Opt out of calls" feature. Any time a contact asks not to be called, you are required to flag them immediately using the steps below. This is your primary action — do not wait, do not delegate it.

📋 Step-by-Step: How to Opt a Contact Out of Calls

1

Open the contact's profile in Apollo

Search by name in People, find them in a sequence task queue, or click directly from a call log.

2

Click the "···" button in the contact header

Located top-right of the contact, next to "Add to list" and "Add to sequence." It opens a short dropdown menu.

3

Select "Opt out of calls"

Apollo immediately suppresses this contact from all call-based sequences and tasks. The action takes effect instantly — no confirmation screen.

4

Notify your manager

Let your team lead know so the opt-out can be flagged at the campaign and list level — ensuring no colleague accidentally calls the same contact.

🚦 Decision Guide: Can I Call This Number?

  • Landline + DNC Registry + EBR within 18 months? → May be OK under TSR. Confirm with compliance.
  • Cell phone + auto-dialer + no written consent? → Do NOT call. TCPA violation risk.
  • Cell phone + documented written consent? → OK to proceed.
  • Any number + internal DNC flag? → Do NOT call. Period.
  • Any number + time zone outside 8 AM–9 PM? → Wait until permitted hours.
  • Not sure? → Escalate to Compliance. Never guess.
